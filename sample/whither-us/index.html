Whither Us

  • #Culture
  • #AI

    • I fully expect this post to age poorly, as would any piece that purports to predict the future of AI. But my confidence in our ability to take care of each other and thrive as a species is faltering.

    AI is only going to be an enormously powerful accelerator of inequality – at least in the short term. While changes will manifest differently from the industrial revolution, the power structures are the same, and the outcome will also be the same.

    AIs that take over the world make for some great storytelling. But now that AI is moving forward at a fever pitch, these worries are starting to look less like fiction.

    Perhaps the logical conclusion1 is correct and we are in fact irredeemable as a species. Before we get wiped out because we are bad custodians of the planet, a few more things are much more likely to happen, like the coming wave of automation.

    What would a world look like if most white-collar jobs have been automated away?

    Can Talk, Can’t Walk

    Unlike what science fiction authors of yore surmised, precise motor skills along with navigating around the world turned out to be more difficult to emulate than tasks that were previously considered to be in the realm of creativity and human nature. We thought there would be dumb humanoid robots doing laundry and cooking for us while us humans wrote poetry and painted sunsets. That’s not how it’s turning out.

    There’s good reason for it. Humans are great at adaptation. Hoofing it around the planet turned out to involve substantial intuition about the world we inhabit. Every moment we maintain a model of the world around us and our own body. Millenia of adaptation and a lifetime of experience informs this model. Replicating such a system is

    Easiest tasks for an information processing machine to do turned out to be spitting out more information. The easiest jobs to automate, in turn, turned out to be in the realm of media generation.

    For millennia humanity humbly curated the most desirable patterns of words, pictures, and sounds. Our time tested method of being good at creating these things is to imitate other good works of art – good artists borrow, great artists steal. Large language models are exceptionally good at picking out patterns in media and replicating them. Pattern recognition and replication is our MO.

    So it comes as no surprise that LLMs have turned out to be exceptional at producing creative works.

    Curate Curate Curate

    However, AIs have one big handicap: they can’t feel. A LLM cannot determine the goodness of some piece of media or a sequence of words. All it can do is to follow the patterns it has seen and elicit the same exclamations it has seen people elicit when presented with similar patterns. For anything new they need humans to feel for them, and the way we feel on their behalf is to continue our sacred duty to curate the best media.

    Thanks to YouTube, TikTok, and others, we have democratized media creation in the past couple of decades. Mass creation of media also leads to the problem of curation and recommendation. After all, nobody has the time to go through everything anymore. So these platforms also created the most effective recommendation systems. The same advantages that machine learning had at recognizing patterns and recreating them came in super handy. In an almost reductionist fashion, these platforms consumed our reactions – the views, and thumbs ups, the hearts – and learned exactly what to present in front of our eyes to keep us engaged. These are the perfect platforms to harvest signals about our preferences. It is likely that – for better or for worse – we will continue to perform this duty of training machines to entertain us, partly because we can’t stop ourselves. We are stuck in a positive feedback loop. Our recommendation systems powering these are just too good.

    Where Is All The Wealth Going?

    But why would an AI continue to entertain us? Because we want it to.

    The next wave of jobs that are the easiest to automate are information processing jobs: tasks where a person ingests some information in some form, and produces more information in another form. It’s crucial that not a lot of reasoning happens in between, because as much as people like to believe we’ve built reasoning machines, we are not quite there yet (Plaat et al., 2024).

    Every economic system in the world unfailingly does one thing well: they saturate labor. They reach equilibrium when labor supply is exhausted – in more ways than one. How these systems differ is in the quality of life that is afforded those who labor.

    John Maynard Keynes said in his seminal essay “Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren” concludes that the economic problem may be solved, […] within a hundred years (Keynes, 1930).

    Given that the essay was written in 1930, it’s close to the 100 year mark since the essay, and the economic problem is very much not solved. To be honest I’d give it another 100 years for the ideals mentioned in the essay to come to fruition. What ideals, you might ask?

    Keynes categorized needs into two broad categories: absolute and relative. Absolute needs are those that we now consider to be inelastic demands – things like housing, food, healthcare. Relative needs are those arising from our appetite to feel superior.

    His expectation was that society will mature to a point where we will share the bounties of the exponentially increasing productivity thus satiating the absolute needs of most everyone. The rich would gradually let go of the need for the accumulation of wealth and grow to be weary of it.

    Disappointingly, our greed for wealth has only increased. The unforeseen obstacle was in fact quite the opposite of what was predicted.

    Instead of sharing in the bounty of the increased productivity, those with wealth chose to extract cheaper labor out of those less fortuanate than them. The mechanism of this was to deprive people of those same essential goods and services that counted as absolute needs, and then ration them out in ways that guarantee a source of income for a few.

    A good example of this mechanism is housing. There are many reasons for the real-estate markets are what they are some rooted in greed and prejudice. But the end result is that no matter how much middle class income grows (it doesn’t grow all that much) real-esstate will capture any surplus. The more one has the more one would be willing to pay for their basic needs. And the situation is not getting all that better (Hermann & Whitney, 2024).

Optimal line-length for publishing on the web

Written by Asanka Herath on June 14, 2023 | #Usability #Curiosity

As you could probably tell from the general look and feel of this site, it derives some amount of inspiration from Medium1. The two important aspects that I find attractive on Medium are:

  1. Typography. The font pairings are easy on the eyes.
  2. Text width. Also easy on the eyes, but for a different reason. It seems to lend itself to easy reading.

Linotype newspapers had narrow columns for practical reasons. Not necessarily the ergonomics of the reader, but rather the publisher.

A metal line of type (“line-o-type” ⇝ linotype) also called a slug becomes difficult to work with if it is too long. In particular, the machines assembled text line-by-line. The process requires that all the lines are of equal width. A small line width – 2 inches in this case – was apparently the right trade-off.

However, it also just so happens that having a narrow column makes it easier to find the start of the next line after reading one. Line spacing helps; double-spacing isn’t just a trick to reach push the page count.

So the goal here is to figure out a width that is short enough that the reader can find the next line comfortably, and long enough to not require excessive effort to read each line. Oh and one more thing; newspapers – or print in general – is different from reading on the web in terms of distance from the eye to the text. That also affects line tracking and saccadian movement.

What’s the optimal line length then?

Bernard et al. (2003) studied this very question. Their study looked at (get it? looked at? haha) three different layouts presented on what would be considered a regular desktop screen.

    Lots of references about screen-reading research. Generally point towards the fact that people read slower on the screen rather than on paper. Not clear if any accounted for the different layouts that are possible on screens.

  • Eye movements during reading: a theory of saccade initiation times https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0042698901000256

    As people read continuous text, on occasional single eye fixations the text was replaced by one of six alternate stimulus patterns. Frequency distributions of the durations of these fixations were used to test predictions from four types of theories of saccadic eye movement control. Contrary to current cognitive theories, cognitive influences appeared to delay saccades rather than trigger them. Two saccade disruption times were identified, suggesting the existence of three distributions of saccades, labeled early, normal and late. The Competition–inhibition theory, an enhanced version of Findlay and Walker’s (1999) theory, is proposed to account for eye movement control during reading.

  • F-Shaped Pattern of Reading on the Web: Misunderstood, But Still Relevant (Even on Mobile) https://www.nngroup.com/articles/f-shaped-pattern-reading-web-content/

    Eyetracking research shows that people scan webpages and phone screens in various patterns, one of them being the shape of the letter F. Eleven years after discovering this pattern, we revisit what it means today.

  • How Little Do Users Read? https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-little-do-users-read/ On the average Web page, users have time to read at most 28% of the words during an average visit; 20% is more likely.

Incentives

Written by Asanka Herath on December 3, 2023 | #

Let’s talk about incentives and psychological safety.

What Matters To You

Typically the actions of an employee within a company depend on their percieved effect on a number of factors. In no specific order, they could be generalized into the following:

  1. The self – a.k.a. self-preservation and self-enrichment.
  2. The company – a.k.a. the entity that controls the payroll. When I say “company”, I’m including the entire structure that produces and shares wealth with the employee. Management structures, co-workers (in their professional roles 1), and other stakeholders all get lumped into “company” for convenience. The employee – whom we are assuming is not very high up in the org-chart – is affected by, but typically has little effect on these entities. Lumping all those entities into the single “company” entity keeps things simple for now.
  3. The customers or users – a.k.a. whom the employees see as they ultimately serve.
  4. Society – a.k.a. among whom the employee lives.
  5. The environment and the planet – a.k.a. everything and everyone else.

When presented with a decision like what to do when something goes wrong, the employee will weigh the percieved effect of their decision on each of these entities. Such a consideration may not happen consciously, especially in the heat of the moment. However over time people tend to build up their own intuition around what works and what doesn’t. The ambient culture has a lot to do with the formation of these intuitions.

It is this intuition that we seek to affect with the culture that we build around ourselves.

It’s safe to assume that in general everyone will hold themselves at the top of this list. That leaves us with how we organize the remainder relative to the self.

In an ideal world, the interests that benefit each of these will have no conflicts. The society in general will seek to look after each other and the environment. Individuals – the customers and users – in turn will seek sustainable goods and services that benefit society in addition to meeting their immediate needs. The company will seek to produce such goods and services; and set up incentives for their employees to do so.

A Culture of Mutual Respect and Safety

One of the more successful concepts at Google is the blameless postmortem. The SRE Book’s section on postmortem culture2 explains it in detail. But the most relevant bit for this article is as follows:

Removing blame from a postmortem gives people the confidence to escalate issues without fear. It is also important not to stigmatize frequent production of postmortems by a person or team. An atmosphere of blame risks creating a culture in which incidents and issues are swept under the rug, leading to greater risk for the organization

A cultural cornerstone the likes of a blameless postmortem naturally guides people towards a way of thinking that is outward facing. Since there is no risk to self an employee is much more likely to act towards the benefit of others. This is especially true if “doing the right thing” is rewarded.

What happens when people feel constantly at risk is easy to see. Faults get swept under the rug, as the SRE book points out. Things that negatively affect the quality of good and services produced will remain unaddressed. Users lose trust in the company. Ultimately everything goes downhill.

Current Events at Google

A well respected engineer with a long tenure, Ian Hixie, recently left Google.

After he left, he wrote a blistering reflection of where Google is heading. You should read it.

One thing that he points out are the results of the recent layoffs at Google:

The effects of layoffs are insidious. Whereas before people might focus on the user, or at least their company, trusting that doing the right thing will eventually be rewarded even if it’s not strictly part of their assigned duties, after a layoff people can no longer trust that their company has their back, and they dramatically dial back any risk-taking. Responsibilities are guarded jealously. Knowledge is hoarded, because making oneself irreplaceable is the only lever one has to protect oneself from future layoffs.

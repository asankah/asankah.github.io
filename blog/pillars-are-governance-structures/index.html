Pillars Are Governance Structures

Written by Asanka Herath on September 8, 2023 | #Culture #Career

Re-orgs.

It’s a fact of life for large organizations. A popular metaphor making the rounds now ,at least for me, is this concept of pillars. What was once called teams or simply referred to by their pronoun-less product names is now called pillars. The metaphor makes sense. Let’s start at the top.

On the top is some kind of goal. The pillars support the goal. There could be some sort of foundation in the bottom but that never seems to come up. The metaphor stops before we get to the bottom I guess.

Now things get complicated. Each pillar has subpillars. (I reduced the number of pillars for clarity.)

The subpillars are holding up the pillars, I guess. All of them coming together to hold up the goal or organization. We are using different words, but nothing is that different from organizations / teams / and sub-teams.

Collectively what we are looking at is a system of organizing people. People can’t seem to agree about how to organize people.

Pillars or teams are organizational structures. “Leaders” of these pillars lay out priorities. They are also the advocates of their respective subpillars and teams.

Or rather they “should” be.

In TeamTopologies Martin Fowler et. al. discusses different kinds of teams. What applies to teams applies to pillars – they are the same thing. What I found compelling was the distinction between Outcome Oriented teams and Activity Oriented teams.

To put it in a nutshell,

  • Outcome oriented teams are teams that are working towards a specific outcome. They hope to achieve some sort of goal, and once it is achieved the teams either dissolve, or delegate out to a activity oriented team.

    Outcome oriented teams have specific measurable goals like build a thing, make specific qualitative change happen, research something, and achieve a sales goal.

  • Activity oriented teams are teams that perform some sort of activity for the organization. They do things like keeping the lights on, bug fixing, and occasional feature development.

    Activity oriented teams have qualitative goals like uptime, efficiency, user-satisfaction, and support level.

Activity oriented teams are typically in the service of enabling an outcome, which would be primarily on the shoulders of an outcome oriented team.

There are more details and nuances and much hair-splitting. But the gist of it can be understood without diving too far into the weeds. What is important is that …

You can’t run a goal oriented team as an activity oriented team and vice versa.

One can’t set up sensible goals if the modalities doesn’t match up. And by modalities I mean the type of team.

And here’s the kicker, the “subpillars” or subteams can support multiple upstream goals. Especially if they are activity oriented teams. So our picture actually looks more like this.

In TeamTopologies parlance, these would be – to simplify things a little bit – enabling teams.

Notice how the foundations aren’t under specific pillars or (vertial) subpillars. The problem with the pillars and subpillars model is that these activity oriented teams don’t fit inside pillars; because they are foundations.

We can, however, stick teams into pillars. These teams would be outcome oriented if they are stuck into outcome oriented pillars. It follows that activity oriented pillars will need to be stuck into activity oriented pillars.

But the gist of it is you can’t plan teams or pillars until you know what their goals are.

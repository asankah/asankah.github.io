An Intuition on Entropy

Shannon entropy is often used to measure the information content of a probability distribution. A common critique is that entropy can be misleading. Let’s see why.

This post skips over an in-depth explanation of Shannon entropy and only addresses the intuition surrounding it and how it can be misleading. Googling for “Shannon entropy” should come up with plenty of options for a more thorohugh treatement of inforamtion theory.

Before that let’s get a couple of definitions out of the way.

Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) be a probability space. I.e.

\[\mathcal{F} = 2^{\Omega}\]

… and …

\[P : \mathcal{F} \mapsto [0,1]\]

The Shannon entropy of the probability function \(P\) is given by:

\[H(P) = \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} P(\omega) \cdot \log_2\left( P(\omega) \right)\qquad(1)\]

So, what does all this mean?
